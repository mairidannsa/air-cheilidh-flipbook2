                                    6. Cruthachas | CreativityFor many people in the Gaelic community, music and other creative arts live at the heart of their cultural and community experience, acting as a touchstone for what it means to live and express oneself in a Gaelic way. Given music%u2019s crosscultural accessibility, it is often the thread which initially pulls people into closer engagement with the culture. A song may captivate a new listener%u2019s ear, or it may rekindle a precious memory of being sung to as a child. Alongside music, Gaelic culture is also enriched by many other forms of creative expression from praise poems to improvised step dancing, tall tales to witty impersonations, cloth to carvings, and so much more. Creative arts wove their way through the series of Air Ch%u00e9ilidh, both in the topics discussed and the music which bookended the sessions. Manysessions began with an archival recording of Nova Scotia Gaelic singers or fiddlers and ended with a sharing from our many musical guests. Participants were encouraged to set aside their self-censorship and draw us into vivid, immersive stories. One of the great things about re-authoring is that, when the process is flowing, people transform into storytellers on the spot. Creating togetherDuring the discussions, participants explored topics such as the connection between fiddle music and the Gaelic language, the intergenerational joy of square dancing, creative writing and filmmaking and the subtle art of Gaelic humour. They thought about how these art forms are often created and shared within the collective: a song improvised around the milling table, a new fiddle tune appreciated by the dancers in a square set, a community in-joke which contains four generations%u2019 worth of history in a single quip. They connect us to a time when Gaels were remarkably self-sufficient in both material culture and entertainment and give us insight into the interplay between individual self-expression and community cohesion within the culture. The gifts of musicIn Season 3, Session 5, fiddlers Shelly Campbell and Glenn Graham spoke of their musical journeys, particularly the support they received from their families and communities and how this influenced their playing style. They spoke of their music both as a cultural inheritance and as a conduit for individual self-expression. Gifts were mentioned many times, whether in the form of physical items, guidance and encouragement from adults or the idea of creative %u201ctalent%u201d received from the Otherworld. Glenn remembered his late grandfather, renowned fiddler Donald Angus Beaton, gifting him a fiddle and bow when he was seven years old. He interpreted the gift as a vote of confidence in his potential and it had a profound effect on him.%u201cIt was a really formative moment,%u201d he recalled, %u201cI could see myself continuing.%u201d He also remembered his mother Mary (n%u00e9e Beaton) guiding him in learning %u201cthe locally accepted norm of what%u2019s correct for a tune.%u201d She explained that if you go beyond the locally accepted %u2018correct%u2019 form the listeners will notice, and she taught him that it was a sign of respect to play the local version %u201ccorrectly.%u201dMany Gaelic women who may not have played instruments themselves nonetheless held detailed and extensive repertoires of tunes in their memory. They passed these on to younger generations in the form of puirt-%u00e1-beul songs or by %u201cjigging%u201d (diddling) the tunes.
