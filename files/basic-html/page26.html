The West Mabou Hall hosts one of Cape Breton%u2019s longest running and most popular family dances, and all three guests have a strong affection for it. Margie MacInnis%u2019s late husband, Jimmy, started the dance in 1988, and it was in that hall that Dale Gillis danced his first set. He%u2019s been hooked ever since. It is the joy of the dancing, and the people, that keeps him coming back. %u201cA square dance is happy music,%u201d he said, %u201cit's got drive to it%u2014I don't always see that in other places.%u201dParticipants on the call agreed: %u201cDale was so right about the happiness effect at the dances, the music, the community%u201d, %u201c[I was moved by] how connected the music, dance and Gaelic language are and how good that feels in my heart, to be part of a deep, beautiful, integrated culture.%u201d Margie MacInnis also spoke of the fun, good exercise and mood-boost a dance could provide. %u201cI met so many nice people,%u201d she said. %u201cSome evenings I wouldn't feel like going but once I would go, I would always have fun. You could guarantee that.%u201dSquare dance in Glencoe [image credit: M%u00e0iri Britton]While there are several adult dances in Cape Breton where alcohol is sold, Margie spoke of the importance of keeping the West Mabou hall open to youth. She shared a poem she wrote for the dances, after Jimmy died suddenly in 2016. Two of the lines speak to this intergenerational importance:People of all ages dancing together, he observed,Leading to our culture and traditions being preserved.%u201cThis has been so wonderful tonight!%u201d one participant commented at the end of the session, %u201cJust thinking about the wonderful spirit in the halls and the great times with friends and family and the music with the floor bouncing. Drive 'er!%u201d Reflecting on the first series of Air Ch%u00e9ilidh, host Mike Kennedy spoke of how he sensed concentric circles of energy lifting at a dance, as the musicians and dancers fed a joyful feedback loop throughout the night. He said he felt the same energy during the Air Ch%u00e9ilidh sessions, as conversations between hosts and guests sparked connections between participants as far removed as Scotland and California, Cape Breton and Brazil. The dance caller%u2019s cry of %u201call join hands%u201d captures something of this spirit of connection and transmission, forming a circle through which to share rhythm, energy and the sense of well-being which comes from driving tunes and joyful, synchronised movement. For the fun of itThe dance hall is an important place where people get to goof off and personalities shine through. Outside the dancehall, people gather in the parking lot to drink and tell stories, often long after the dance has ended.
