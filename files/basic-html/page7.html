                                    1. Ath-sgeulachadh | Re-storying%u201cTake us to a moment%u2026%u201dThere%u2019s often a moment, isn%u2019t there? Maybe you sensed it as it happened, or maybe the awareness comes with hindsight, but either way you realise it was the start of something significant.In June 2018, a special blanket passed from the hands of its weaver %u00d2mar Bhochanan | Amber Buchanan, a young Gaelic woman from the North Shore of Cape Breton, and into the hands of Ma%u2019git Poulette, an Elder from the Mi%u2019kmaw community of We'koqma'q. They were at the first How We Thrive event in Halifax, organised and convened by a group of Nova Scotians. Chen%u00e9 Swart and Sobaz Benjamin had co-led a module called %u201cShaping Our Future Through Stories%u201d where participants from Mi%u2019kmaq, Gaelic, African Nova Scotian, newcomer and other communities had come together to deepen their sense of connection through story-sharing. As the session drew to aclose Amber presented Ma%u2019git with the cloth from her loom. %u201cKnowing I was coming here and that I would meet my Mi%u2019kmaw neighbours,%u201d she said, %u201cI spun some yarn and wove this cloth in the five colours of the Medicine Wheel. I felt ashamed that I had grown up just down the road from your community but never really met any of these neighbours. I wanted to express my gratitude for having this chance to be together here, and to offer you this gift.%u201dSusan Szpakowski remembers the significance of this exchange: %u201cThat moment was a gift to us all,%u201d she wrote. %u201cLooking back, I realize that my passion about narrative was fueled by a desire for more moments like this one. I was willing to commit time and energy to learning more about how such moments happen, what conditions are needed. Because heaven knows we need more stories that acknowledge and begin to heal colonial wounds.%u201d The seed for The Narrative Project had been planted.%u00d2mar and Ma%u2019git at How We Thrive, 2018Made of storiesTwo years later, following another rich in-person gathering, Susan convened a team that would host a series of online conversations. These would feature facilitators and guests from around the Atlantic provinces who would explore narratives they wanted to %u201cre-author%u201d and those they wanted to celebrate. It was an eclectic group, and it worked. Susan remembers how %u201cnone of the rest of us would have thought of the stories that the others brought forward. And so it was always surprising, and a little scary, and also beautiful.%u201dIt also couldn%u2019t have come at a better time. Just as the series was scheduled to start, making use of a little-known online platform called Zoom, the world ground to a halt as a result of the COVID-19 pandemic.
