                                    4. T%u00ecr | LandAt the beginning of every Air Ch%u00e9ilidh call, visitorswere asked to write in the chat where they were calling from. It was a way of mapping communities across the globe and connecting everyone from the first moment. One of the very special aspects of these online gatherings was that they brought together people from all over the world with an interest in, or connection to, Gaelic. Our team was based in Nova Scotia, but the gathering included folks from across Canada and the United States, Scotland, and several other countries including Brazil. Concepts of land, home, lineage and belonging meant different things for different people. But since all our hosts, and most of our guests and participants, were from either Nova Scotia or Scotland, much of the conversations centred around the dynamics of land, indigeneity, colonization and right-relations within those two places.Where we standEach session of Air Ch%u00e9ilidh started with a Land Acknowledgment, recognising Nova Scotia%u2019s place within Mi%u2019kma%u2019ki, the sacred ancestral and unceded land of the Mi%u2019kmaq First Nation. We named the Mi%u2019kmaq as the past, present and future caretakers of the land. In Gaelic, we used the phrase %u201cbha c%u00e0irdeas agus co-roinneadh eadarainn o shean | there has been friendship and sharingbetween us for a long time.%u201d This was intended notas a sugar-coating of past and present injustices,but to honour the generosity shown particularlyby the Mi%u2019kmaq towards the Gaels when manyarrived displaced and destitute from Scotlandaround 200 years ago. It is undeniable that manyGaels would not have survived their first year(s) inNova Scotia without the help of the Mi'kmaq andtheir deep knowledge of the landscape.Being a Gael in Nova Scotia is to hold together an Indigenous cultural lineage connecting back to the land in Scotland, and a settler identity within Canada. It is to belong to a people both colonized and colonizing, with all the nuance and complexity of each individual%u2019s experience of that history. It is to be governed by the Treaties of Peace and Friendship, signed by representatives of the British/Canadian Governments and the Mi%u2019kmaq.Is Luchd-c%u00f9mhnaint sinn uileWe are all Treaty PeopleFrom Season 2 onwards, each season of Air Ch%u00e9ilidh included a session dedicated to listening to and building relationships with our Mi%u2019kmaw neighbours. In one of these sessions (Season 2, Session 7) Mi%u2019kmaw guest, Sa%u2019n Sulian | John William %u201cTiny%u201d Cremo, spoke of the urgent issue of housing shortages within Mi%u2019kmaw communities in Nova Scotia and how a key reason for this is that following decades of settlement by colonizers, they simply do not have enough land to build on. Host Frances MacEachen articulated the irony of us joining so many others in starting our events with a land acknowledgment, when the Mi%u2019kmaqhave so little land.Knowing the landThere is much to consider within the stories of colonialism, past and present, in Scotland and in Nova Scotia. Alongside the Mi%u2019kmaw experience, participants also considered the Gaels as Indigenous people to Scotland, displaced and oppressed through a series of injustices including the Highland Clearances. Participants pondered the teachings held in Gaelic culture that can help us to live in right-relationship with the land and each other. We talked about how our ancestors, wherever they were from, connected to their home places and all the life forms there, and how we might do the same today.Through the seasons of Air Ch%u00e9ilidh, participantsbegan a process of coming to know the land through Indigenous Gaelic and Mi%u2019kmaw perspectives. In Season 4, Session 3, I (M%u00e0iri) hosted a session and invited my friend Ceit | KateLanghorne to be my guest.
