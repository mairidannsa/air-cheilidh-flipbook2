For various reasons, many Gaelic speakers seem to have internalized a message that it is rude to speak their language if there%u2019s even one person present who doesn%u2019t understand it. How did this narrative take hold? Perhaps it stems from the Gaelic value of wanting people to feel included and able to converse:Cha%u2019n fhiach cuirm gun a c%u00f2mhradhA feast is worth nothing without its conversationBut it was felt by many to also be a direct result of colonization, as Gaelic was forced into subordination to English. In Season 3, Session 3, host Iain MacLeod and guests Robert Pringle and Donelda McComb considered the nuances of different linguistic spaces and what it means to exist in a minority language community within a wider geographic and cultural community. We must navigate different roles and contexts which may not always feel the most natural or comfortable, but there is much to be gained through developing the courage to reclaim lost aspects of one%u2019s identity. %u201cIt feels unnatural at times,%u201d Robert acknowledged, %u201cbut as a learner in Nova Scotia you have to don this [Gaelic mantle]. You have to put it on. It was taken away from us, it was unnaturally removed.%u201d One participant commented that %u201cthis is a story of multiple generations not feeling safe as a minority andlearning that they had to be %u2018polite%u2019 to not be targeted, and hopefully the culture has changed enough that this will no longer be the reality.%u201dRe-authoring the narrative that %u201cit%u2019s rude to speak a language around people who don%u2019t understand%u201dmight look like making sure to welcome everyone to a space in the language they understand (a practice used by the organizers of Nova Scotia Gaels%u2019 Jam events) but then finding ways to encourage and support learners in a Gaelic-only environment. Te%u00e0rlach spoke of subtly ensuring that Gaelic becomes %u201cthe unmarked code of the space%u201d at the Taigh C%u00e9ilidh Caf%u00e9, using props to help people order their drinks in Gaelic and offering a 10% discount as a fun encouragement for doing so. He noticed that through doing this, the distinctions between learners and native speakers begin to blur: %u201cyou just have people who need to communicate in this unmarked code.%u201d Gaelic is suddenly not the minority anymore, which is immensely validating for Gaels who are otherwise surrounded by constant reminders of how their language is sidelined in mainstream life.Robert also spoke of how he tries to weave Gaelic through his everyday interactions: %u201cI drop sentences at the store. I say little greetings to people who may not speak Gaelic. I do it with affection and with joy.%u201d Donelda acknowledged that oftentimes people will switch from Gaelic to English to accommodate an English-speaker without confirming that hearing only Gaelic bothers them: %u201cwe don't know how the person felt, because we very readily went into English.
