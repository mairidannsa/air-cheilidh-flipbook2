MacLeod, MacKillop and MacIver were common family names on the island, and thepeople there felt a strong affinity to the Norse part of their heritage, which dates to before the Middle Ages. %u201cHe would speak about them as if they were his cousins, like he knew them when he was young,%u201d she recalls.When Stacey returned to Berneray many years later, after Norman had passed away, she was dismayed that it no longer felt so much like home. %u201cWhen I went back, I expected to go home, and it was because those people weren't there, especially him, that it didn't feel like home.%u201d There is a good kind of heartache, she said, which reminds you that you loved something or someone deeply and it helps to keep the memory of them alive. %u201cThere are no monuments to these people,%u201d she said. %u201cIt's so important that when you talk about them, you are honouring them and memorializing them, not in a way that is a burden, but a celebration.%u201dThe connections of friendship and apprenticeship between Shannon and Anna and Stacey and Norman moved and inspired many who were listening on the call. These recorded voices are like the echo of a visit%u2014they let us visit with the likes of E%u00f2s N%u00ecll Bhig | Joe Neil MacNeil or Mairead Bean Ruairidh | Margaret MacLean even if we never knew them when they were alive.In the very first session of Air Ch%u00e9ilidh, Gaelic folklorist, Dr. John Shaw, spoke of his memories from fieldwork trips in Cape Breton in the 1970s and %u201980s. He remembered, %u201cI found the older people, the Gaelic speakers, had a beautiful social style. You could visit people you didn%u2019t know and within a few minutes you were relaxed. They were very easy to be with.
