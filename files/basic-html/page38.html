                                    Dr. John Shaw, Frances MacEachen & Lili WatsonGaelic singer and archivist, Mary Jane Lamond, embodies the kind of collective growth which has been taking place in Nova Scotia in recent decades. She learned Gaelic to fluency as a young adult and has gone on to record six albums featuring songs she learned from tradition-bearers in their kitchens, or by listening to their recorded voices. In her conversation with Shannon MacMullin in Season 4, Session 1, Mary Jane returned many times to the theme of learning and what the process of listening diligently and attentively to Gaelic archive recordings has taught her. She recalled the effort she went to to seek out these recordings before they were available online, making the trek to the Nova Scotia Archives in Halifax with a copy of Gaelic Songs of Nova Scotiato listen to the cassette tapes in the Helen Creighton Collection. She felt she was in over her head, like she knew nothing, but she loved the voices: %u201cI could feel the joy that they had in singing, what that was physically doing to them.%u201dWhen it came to learning to speak Gaelic, both Mary Jane and Shannon commented on how invaluable the archive recordings have been for them, and how listening to them became a social process as they gathered with friends for listening parties. Shannon described how these were like a frolic %u201cconnecting back and decoding what%u2019s in those songs.%u201d Mary Jane felt that the voices were %u201creaching out to us and giving us a visit, in a way.%u201dHome now after more than two decades touring as a professional musician, for the past seven years Mary Jane has been a key member of the team behind the Cainnt is Ceathramhan | Language and Lyrics project, directed by Dr. Heather Sparling at Cape Breton University. The project has created an index of the Gaelic songs made or known in Nova Scotia, and is preparing to release a re-catalogued and transcribed database of the Cape Breton Gaelic Folklore Collection, collected by Dr. John Shaw. Pondering the subject of memory, Mary Jane marveled at how the Cape Breton Gaelic FolkloreCollection is made up of over 2,000 recordings from around 250 informants, with most of the recordings coming from just 54 individuals. As an example of these Gaels%u2019 incredible recall, she told us of a visit with her neighbour Seonaidh Aonghais Bhig | Johnny Williams where Johnny had sung perfectly several verses of the complex song Cead Deireannach nam Beann (composed by Donnchadh B%u00e0n Mac an t-Saoir) despite only hearing the song a couple of times in his life, aged 13. Mary Jane also pointed out, though, that many tradition bearers practiced their story-telling and singing in private to hone their craft, and she expressed an intention to reinstate a more regular practice in her own daily life as well.As projects like Cainnt is Ceathramhan, Tobar an Dualchais and DASG develop more accessible finding aides and transcriptions to help people access the archives, the possibilities expand for more listening frolics, quiet digital visits and the memorization and sharing of songs, stories and lore. This will hopefully prompt other, perhaps dormant, memories to rise to the surface as well. As John said, %u201cIf a lot of people are coming together and concentrating on a tradition, they are going to remember a lot that is buried in their own minds from their families.%u201d This becomes a way to honour the legacies of those who came before us, the people for whom, as Stacey noted, there are no monuments. As one participant commented, %u201cWe are their monuments when we remember.%u201d We need only put on headphones and press the play button to know they are still singing with us.33
