They spoke of how the familiarity of generation upon generation growing up together creates an intimate knowing that allows people to mimic each other with impressive and often hilarious accuracy. Peter mentioned that if he sees an idiosyncrasy in someone, he will often practice it and possiblyborrow it for one of his character sketches. Katie added that a person might spend an hour in church looking at the back of another person%u2019s head, a practice of paying attention which has translated into her highly evocative drawings.Katie acknowledged that there might be an element of this kind of humour that seems %u201cexclusive%u201d and hard for outsiders to understand.But she explained that %u201cpeople are making jokes for each other, for ourselves,%u201d they are not necessarily thinking about how to perform to, or entertain, a wider audience. This is a humour rooted in a Gaelic sensibility, the same sensibility which shapes humorous songs about neighbours and funny events that happen in the community. It is a tradition which continues today in Nova Scotia with friends composing puirt%u00e1-beul for each other on their birthdays. Participants voiced the loss that they felt from having grown up without their native language, and the healing that came from starting to reclaim it. Far from being %u201cirrelevant,%u201d as the mainstream narrative often implies, these minority languages are ecosystems of the mind, offering wisdom and creative inspiration to those who explore them.
