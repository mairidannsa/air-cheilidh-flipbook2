                                    Narrative Project team. %u201cI remember those sessions as being really inspirational,%u201d she said, %u201cbecause it was a diverse team of people from different regions.%u201d Frances hosted a session with Mi%u2019kmaw elder and master basket-weaver Ma'lklit | Margaret Gould Pelletier and young Gael, Abaidh Dh%u00f2mhnullach | Abigail MacDonald. Ma%u2019lklit spoke of how her family had lost their home during Centralization, when Mi%u2019kmaw communities were forcefully amalgamated into reserves like Eskasoni in Cape Breton. She endured many difficult years in Residential School, but her parents%u2019 resilience and determination to pass on their language and culture meant that she learned her mother tongue and ancestral skills such as basket weaving.Descended from Scottish Gaelic and Italian immigrants, Abi is working hard to reclaim her ancestral Gaelic language and is a talented musician and songwriter. She shared a song about her grandmother which she had written in Gaelic with her uncle, Goiridh D%u00f2mhnullach | Jeff MacDonald, who is a fluent Gaelic-speaker. Frances reflected on how ancestral gifts, both baskets and song-making, emerged organically as the central theme of the session, and how wellreceived it was by participants on the call. %u201cIt got me thinking, %u201cWe should do something for the Gaelic community%u2019 . . . I was feeling we, as Gaels,tend to be held back by our stories . . . so it would be helpful to unpack some of those and say, Arethey true? Do they resonate with us? Are they helpful? Are there other stories that we can choose?%u201dTaking timeThis led to the first season of Air Ch%u00e9ilidh | the Gaelic Narrative Project, which took place in winter 2021. A small team of hosts adapted How We Thrive%u2019s 90-minute template structure which guided each session:~ Welcome with music~ 10 mins break-out room with groups of three (3 mins each to talk)~ 35 mins conversation between host and guest~ 20 mins breakout group with groups of three (6 mins each to talk)~ Harvest %u2013 everyone typing reflections into the chat~ Wrap up with musicThe breakout rooms and harvest sharing were crucial aspects of the sessions. As Frances commented, %u201cThe more diversity and the more stories in the room . . . [it] gives you greater insight into a bigger story . . . It %u2018thickens%u2019 the narrative.%u201d Each person was encouraged to take their time and to use the full three or six minutes allotted to them to talk, or to allow silence until the words came. Susan added that %u201cwitnessing is so important. Collectively, you're honoring the quiet moment that that person has created for everyone and turning it around so that it is an opportunity and not any kind of failure that they didn't have their words together in time, or that someone else in the group didn't jump in to help them.%u201dBringing this kind of mindfulness to the sessions allowed for moments of surprising insight. Michael White, one of the founders of the Dulwich Centre and a key thinker behind the concept of reauthoring, described a process he called %u201cexoticizing the domestic%u201d%u2014slowing down and considering the mundane in new ways until its familiar contours shift into something quite remarkable. %u201cIn every session, we invite curiosity, listening and surprise,%u201d Susan explained. %u201cWe ask questions that don%u2019t have quick or easy answers.%u201d As a result, %u201csomething fresh always comes up, because the questions invite you to just go to that first thought, memory, moment, feeling or impression, and share that right on the spot. Often, the person who's telling the story learns as much about the question as the person who's listening. [It gets us] out of these thin, dominant, go-to, expedient stories that keep us trapped in business as usual and thinking as usual and our identities as usual.%u201d3
