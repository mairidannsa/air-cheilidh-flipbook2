The image of %u2018bho ghl%u00f9in gu gl%u00f9in | from knee to knee%u2019 makes so much sense.%u201dTheresa MacLellan & Shelly CampbellShelly told the story of how Theresa MacLellan%u2019s family received the gift of music. Her father, %u201cBig Ronald%u201d MacLellan, was a blacksmith who was so strong it was said he could twist a horseshoe around with his bare hands. He was also a renowned fiddler and believed that his creative abilities came from a special fiddle bow which had been fashioned from a fairy arrow found and gifted to him by his aunt. This motif appears in many Gaelic stories and offers a way of thinking about creativity which reflects the close presence of the Otherworld and the role of magical thinking in Gaelic consciousness. This contrasts with a more Anglocentric, post-Renaissance view of human %u2018genius%u2019 as being something innate within special individuals, best accessed through periodsof anguished isolation. The Gaelic worldview offers a way to reframe the narrative around creativity and inspiration, seeing it as an energy which exists just beyond our human realm, and which might visit any one of us if we are lucky and paying attention.And whilst these kinds of magical fairy gifts may be unusual events, their ripple effect can be felt through the generations. Theresa MacLellan absorbed the music from her father and inspired it in Shelly who is now a celebrated player and dedicated, inspirational teacher herself. One participant at Air Ch%u00e9ilidh acknowledged the time and effort that goes into honing one's creative skills. %u201cThank you, Shelly and Glenn,%u201d they said, %u201cfor your inspiration and all the practicing that brought you to the place you are.%u201d Many other comments echoed this sentiment: a recognition that the dedication and generosity embodied by many musicians is a magic in and of itself.All join hands!Another realm in which the community comes together across the generations is at square dances. These social dances rose to prominence during the 1930s when community halls were built and French quadrilles, popular in New England at the time, were brought back to Gaelic communities by people returning from working away. Each community developed distinct sets and after ceding to the craze of %u201cPig %u2019n%u2019 Whistle%u201ddances in the 1970s, square dancing made a comeback in the late 1980s. The dance can be seen as a kind of metaphor for the way individual creativity and established traditions support each other in Gaelic culture.
