                                    Tara spoke of how, as a child, she would visit plants and animals the same way she visited her neighbours. She still feels the importance of visiting Mother Earth. She hikes regularly yearround: %u201cthe longer I hike the more connected I feel, that I%u2019m with her.%u201dThis struck a chord with listeners on the call, with one participant commenting, %u201cI don%u2019t visit Mother Nature enough. I%u2019m overdue for some quality time with her.%u201d Another observed how %u201cland and landscape are important to so many people. A source of comfort. You can understand the longing that some emigrants felt when they never were able to return to their landscapes.%u201d Of course, it was not just emigrants who felt this cianalas | homesickness. From Tara and Bradley Murphy%u2019s other guest, Sarah Prosper, we learned a little of the devastating impacts of Centralization, when Mi%u2019kmaq were driven from their lands and onto reserves in Nova Scotia in the 1940s. The lands they were forced to leave either remained with the Crown or were bought by settlers, the repercussions of which are being felt strongly today, and are yet to be properly reconciled.This theme was also explored in Season 3, Session 2, when host Frances MacEachen spoke to three other Mi%u2019kmaw women%u2014Susan Copage and sisters Ella and Eva Nicholas%u2014about their healing and reclamation work around land, language and culture. \%u201cWe've been so connected to the land, and now we are disconnected. We are in reserves, and we don't have access to the land like our ancestors once did.%u201d Despite this, Eva has developed detailed knowledge of basket weaving and plant medicine, learning from a Mi%u2019kmaw Elder who learned from Eva%u2019s own great-grandmother. Eva shared some of this knowledge on a plant walk as part of a special in-person gathering organised by The Gaelic Narrative Project and the We'koqma'q communityin the summer of 2022.When Frances asked, in our online gathering, what settler people like the Gaels could do to support the Mi'kmaq in reclaiming their language and traditions, Eva%u2019s answer was gracious and meaningful. %u201cTake part in our events,%u201d she suggested. %u201cCome learn, ask questions, just come learn about our culture and history from our narrative, from our perspective, because what has been taught has not been from our perspective and narrative.%u201d She also reflected on how openingup access to the land and waterways is important so the Mi%u2019kmaq can collect medicine. %u201cPerhaps someone has an ash tree, birch tree, or even porcupines that could be used in crafts,%u201d she said, and expressed how it is nice to get an invitation to visit peoples%u2019 land. And so emerged a simple, tangible way to connect and support new stories: a%u2019 dol air ch%u00e9ilidh | going to visit each other on the land. The comments rang out with gratitude for these invitations and peoples%u2019 intention to visit more.
