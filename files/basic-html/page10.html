Aoigheachd | HospitalityF%u00e0ilt%u2019 is furan, ur beatha an taigh!Welcome and welcome, welcome into the house!The name for the Gaelic Narrative Project, Air Ch%u00e9ilidh, comes from one of the most central institutions of Gaelic culture, the c%u00e9ilidh or visit. Hospitality is a deeply embedded value in Gaelic culture, reinforced and celebrated through proverbs, songs, stories and daily practices. It could be a cup of tea and neighbourly chat at the kitchen table or a seven-hour story-telling session and house party to which most of the community shows up. Whatever the occasion, it has long been a Gaelic practice to greet strangers and friends alike with warmth and generosity, offering tea and nourishment. Giving a good welcome is important, and it was a central focus for us in hosting the Gaelic Narrative Project sessions as well. The hosts wanted guests to feel at ease as they arrived into the online space%u2014like they were visiting, in a virtual kitchen. A key component of this was the break-out rooms which allowed participants to share thoughts and stories in small groups, of three or four, at least twice during each session. Many participants commented on how they appreciated %u201cconnecting with community, listening to the variety of speakers sharing from their hearts, and being able to participate despite being so far away%u201d or how they were grateful for the %u201cencouragement and hospitality%u201d given to them.The gifts of the visit%u201cRadical hospitality%u201d is something of a buzzword, but Season 3, Session 6 considered the ways in which Gaels have been embodying this way of being for generations. Host Kieran Walker was joined by guests %u00d2mar Buchanan | Amber Buchanan and Te%u00e0rlach Wilson, themselves skilled and generous hosts in their working and day-today lives. This session considered and re-authoreda modern narrative that no one has time for informal visits these days, and that spontaneous visits, especially if they seem to put %u201cpressure%u201d on the host to provide refreshments, are an imposition. What emerged instead from the various conversations was how much people are yearning for the connection of the visit, and how good it feels to feed and give care and kindness to people.%u00d2mar shared how she felt %u201cthe Gaels really understand our interconnectedness as humans, as people, and the visit remembers [and] practices that interconnection.%u201d She remembered how her great-grandmother, M%u00f2r ni%u2019n Mhurchaidh Dh%u00f2mhnaill | Sadie Mae MacInnis, taught her the etiquette of hosting, including how you must offer a guest tea at least three times before accepting a refusal. By the third time, they have usually gotten over their shyness and said yes! Sadie demonstrated the gift that is simply offering one%u2019s time and presence to be with another. She would drop everything if someone came to visit, giving them her focused and generous attention alongside the obligatory tea and snack. Indeed, the latter was really just a tangible symbol for the former%u2014as %u00d2mar said, when she offered tea %u201cshe was offering me her love.%u201dWhen %u00d2mar took her own daughter to visit in the community, older women would often give little Sadie a brooch or necklace. You rarely left a Gaelic person%u2019s house without a little something to take with you. As %u00d2mar said, %u201cthat radical generosity is part of radical hospitality as well. There%u2019s something very old and wise about this. There%u2019s some kind of magic and healing that happens when we are radically kind to each other.%u201dIn Season 2, Session 6, Nova Scotia Gael, Carman Nic Artair | Carmen MacArthur, spoke lovingly of the hospitality embodied by her neighbour and mentor, the late M%u00e0iri ni%u2019n Ailig Bhig Alasdair %u00d2ig | Mary MacKinnon. %u201cShe got as much joy out of watching you eating her baking as you did eating it,%u201d said Carmen. %u201cShe had a freezer in her basement that was just for baking.
