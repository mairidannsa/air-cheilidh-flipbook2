                                    3. %u00d9ine | TimeHow do we find peace, presence and productivity within the flow of busy days? What do we do when our edges become frayed, and we feel we are out of sync with our natural rhythms? Questions around how we understand and navigate time surfaced in various conversations throughout the seasons of Air Ch%u00e9ilidh. We wondered how Gaelic and Indigenous wisdom might help us find more sustainable, supportive rhythms in our daily lives. Air Ch%u00e9ilidh sessions prompted us to think about different ways of viewing the passage of our lives%u2014as non-linear, as connected to generations past and present%u2014and how the %u201ctiming%u201d inherent within music and dance traditions can give us a sense of presence and connection that is deeply nourishing.Each session of Air Ch%u00e9ilidh began with a lengthy welcome to help everyone arrive and feel grounded before moving into the first round of breakout rooms. Within those smaller group conversations, each participant was encouraged to speak for as much of their allotted time as they felt able and be respectful of not talking through someone else%u2019s time. Many of us are not used to conversations where both speech and silence are held by the collective, and where we can allow a thought to percolate slowly without worrying that we will be interrupted. Equally, our horror of the %u201cawkward silence%u201d is such that we have trained ourselves to be constantly thinking about what we might say next, compromising our ability to listen deeply in the moment. Different and fumbly as it may have been at times, participants consistently commented on how much they appreciated the opportunity to connect and share with others in this more mindful way.There is timeThe pressure to rush%u2014through conversations, through difficult tasks, through days, through life%u2014was a theme which seemed to resonate with many throughout the seasons of Air Ch%u00e9ilidh. Participants acknowledged the changes that have occurred even in the past few decades, and lamented how fast-paced our lives have become. This was a story that could be re-authored by reorienting ourselves to the wisdom of older generations.In Season 2, Session 3, guest Rodney MacDonald spoke to host Mike Kennedy about his upbringing in Mabou, rural Cape Breton, and how he felt the generations who raised him had a different kind of attitude towards, and respect for, time. He explained that his father isn%u2019t a Gaelic-speaker %u201cbut he worked hard, and he had a very Gaelic way of living about him. He liked to do everything by hand.%u201d He remembered a time when he was helping his father dig a hole and wondering why they weren%u2019t using a machine like their neighbours. %u201cWe have the time,%u201d his father said,%u201cwe have enough, we%u2019re not in a panic.%u201d Looking back, Rodney appreciated how the physical, demanding work also gave them precious time together, working side-by-side as father and son, which the machine would have taken away.Spending time in multi-generational spaces can provide a very tangible sense of time through the memories and experiences of each age group. In Season 1, Session 5, Colin Watson talked of what he had learned about time from Migi John H. MacNeil, who was mentor to him as well as Colin%u2019ssister, Lili. He explained how Migi kept a detailed mental log of events that happened from the 1930s onwards, within his own lifetime, and how he %u201ckeeps time%u201d with the generations who were around before he was born. %u201cHe has a great way of keeping track of history that way,%u201d Colin said. In Season 2, Session 3, Angus Brendan MacNeil from Barra talked of how he often thinks of his own family going back six generations and wonders, were they all gathered in one room, who would get on, what could they learn from one another and what would be the older generations%u2019opinion on the present day.
