He talked of the distinctive timing inherent in Nova Scotian Gaelic music and how he first developed a felt sense of this as a child standing on his mother%u2019s feet as she danced to fiddle tunes in the kitchen. This thread of intergenerational transmission continued with his own children. Watching his mother take her grandchildren onto her feet again to teach them to dance gave Rodney a feeling of deep contentment.In addition to a sense that life was generally slower in previous decades or centuries, many people talk of the feeling that time speeds up within our own lives as we get older, regardless of how busy we are. Another year has passed in what feels like the blink of an eye. In music, however, regardless of language, time is allowed to exist within a perpetual present comprised of rhythm and pulse. Experiencing the lift and flow of Gaelic music takes us %u201cout of time,%u201d as in the many stories of people who go into the s%u00ecthean | fairy hill and emerge years later thinking they have only been there a few minutes. Whether it is through music, timebending fairy dwellings, work with our hands or the simple absorption of a good visit, the Gaelic worldview offers us opportunities to rethink our relationship to time and resist the modern pressure to rush the rich experience of our lives.The gift of presenceMany of us feel we don%u2019t %u201chave time%u201d for many of the things we consider important in our lives, like visiting. %u201cHow do we make the time?%u201d %u00d2mar Buchanan asked in Season 3, Session 6, %u201cI think with great intentionality, really.%u201d In response, many listeners commented how they were resolving to slow down and to visit more. One participant said %u201cthe theme of going slower really resonated with us. My mom moved home to Cape Breton seven years ago and I moved my family back seven months ago, both of us with the intent of reconnecting with our communities and culture. Never considered that the %u2018slowing down%u2019 would be a part of that too!%u201dMar a%u2019s gainn%u2019 am biadh, %u2019s ann a%u2019s fial%u2019 a roinnThe scarcer the food, the more generous to share itThis proverb is perhaps as equally applicable to time as to food%u2014giving someone your undivided attention when they come to visit is the modern equivalent of laying out the finest baking, a mark of the same care and dedication in a world hellbent on distracting us at every turn. Gathering together for Air Ch%u00e9ilidh gave many of us a reassuring reminder that there is enough time for the things that matter. We can put our feet in the grass. We can sit with family.
