There are salvage and conservation efforts to try to save and fix in time what has come before. And alongside them, there are revitalisation efforts to try to boost speaker numbers, expand vocabulary and increase capacity in the language. Both are important, butfor much of the 20th century there was perhaps more of a focus on the former than the latter. As Joyce points out, we need to live, share and allow Gaelic to nourish us in real time if we are to ensure its continued presence as a living language.Plant your own potatoesWe can sustain Gaelic, and it will continue to sustain us. This was an abiding and inspiring narrative which emerged from the conversation between guests Anne B. Ryan and Mary Jane Lamond as they spoke to host Frances MacEachen in Season 2, Session 2. They highlighted the resilience and self-sufficiency present within the Gaelic culture, and how it can equip us with tools and values we need to navigate the challenges of our modern lives.Mary Jane commented that, until the 1890s and the arrival of the railway, much of people%u2019s daily lives in Cape Breton were built around a cashless economy of hunting, growing food, building and bartering for supplies. This has changed rapidly in the last 130 years, but the land still offers abundance to those who work with it. While the globalized world and its complexities can sometimes feel overwhelming, we considered ways to simplify and localise our decision-making and our impact. Small-scale food security and building projects, guided by the principles of reciprocity and gu le%u00f2r, can help us stay grounded and well-resourced. Mary Jane shared the simple and wise advice of her neighbour to start small and close to home: %u201cYou just need to plant your own potatoes!%u201dNourished by homeMary Jane also spoke of the importance of %u201cknowing one%u2019s place,%u201d not as a limitation, but in the sense of anxiety-soothing groundedness and belonging. Speaking of friend and respected Gaelic elder, Peter Jack MacLean, she commented how %u201che just knew his place in the world . . . there was no kind of angst about it.%u201d Sometimes, though, it helps to venture further afield to fully appreciate what sustains you from home. This arose as a key theme in Season 1, Session 6 and Season 4, Session 4 as Kenneth MacKenzie, Kate Beaton and Peter MacInnisreflected on how their time spent travelling and living abroad has shaped their sense of home. All three grew up in Mabou but have worked and travelled to many other parts of the world. Peterreflected that it was only when he returned from working for a spell in England that it really hit him how the people in his community have a particular accent and unique mannerisms, and how significant this was to the humour of the place.
