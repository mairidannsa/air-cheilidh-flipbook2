                                    8. Ionnsachadh | LearningSince the start of the 20th century, when Gaelic was spoken by an estimated 100,000 people in Nova Scotia, the language has dwindled to around 2,000 speakers, a decline of 98%. One of the strongest and most pervasive narratives surrounding Gaelic during the last century has been that it is ill-suited to the demands of an increasingly globalised, technological and financially focused world. Many internalised the belief that, at best, Gaelic would do nothing to improve your economic and social situation, and,at worst, it would actively hinder your chances. Parents were told explicitly that speaking their native language to their children would hold them back and damage their developing grasp of English, %u201cthe language of the future.%u201d Children were admonished and punished for speaking Gaelic in school%u2014sometimes by teachers who were Gaelic-speakers themselves%u2014and parents began to use Gaelic as a code language to talk over the heads of the children. The next generation grew up with an English speech cadence still singing with Gaelic inflections, but for most the language was an echo, like a melody you can hear in your head but cannot sing aloud.There have always been those who resisted, but for a while it felt they were swimming against a riptide. In the past few decades, though, a combination of personal healing, collective advocacy and remarkable determination havetransformed the narrative around Gaelic in Nova Scotia. It is now well understood that secondlanguage acquisition (of any kind) improves, rather than limits, cognitive development, and Nova Scotia Gaels have successfully campaigned to establish Gaelic in the school curriculum. There isan independent Gaelic-medium primary school, the first of its kind in North America, and Gaelic representation in government, on the radio and in the arts. Perhaps most importantly, Gaelic is being spoken again more widely in homes and at community gatherings around the province. This is due in no small part to a collection of mentorship programs and the devoted and passionate individuals who have made them a success.An t-ionnsachadh b%u00f2idheach | The beautiful learningThe Bun is B%u00e0rr | Root and Branch program is funded by Gaelic Affairs Nova Scotia andencompasses three streams of mentorship: %u201cNa Gaisgich %u00d2ga | The Young Heroes%u201d for youth aged 8-16, delivered by Colaisde na G%u00e0idhlig | The Gaelic College; %u201cDaltachas | Fostership,%u201d a predominately online offering for intermediate learners, and the eponymous %u201cBun is B%u00e0rr%u201dprogram which pairs advanced learners with Gaelic elders/native speakers to form lasting bonds of learning and friendship. Many of the individuals involved in Air Ch%u00e9ilidh as hosts, guests and participants have benefitted from one or more of these programs. Pairings such as Migi John H. and Colin Watson (Season 1, Session 5), Mary MacKinnon and Carmen MacArthur (Season 2, Session 6) or Shannon MacMullin and Anna MacKinnon (Season 3, Session 7) demonstrate the remarkable growth of linguistic and cultural fluency the program can nurture, not to mention the abiding affectionwhich often develops between the participants.
