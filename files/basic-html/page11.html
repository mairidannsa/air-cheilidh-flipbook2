We feel her generous spirit among us still.A' leigeil anail | Spaces to breatheBoth %u00d2mar and Te%u00e0rlach work hard to create Gaelic gathering spaces which feel safe and welcoming for everyone, including %u201cminorities within the minority,%u201d such as LGBTQIA+ and neurodivergent individuals. Despite the inherent value of hospitality at the heart of Gaelic culture, we heard how many people do not feel automatically welcomed into traditional cultural spaces. Te%u00e0rlach lives and works on the Hebridean island of Lewis on the west coast of Scotland. It has been his experience that every Pride event in his town is accompanied by a counter-protest of ardent Free Presbyterians %u201ctelling us that we%u2019re wrong and we%u2019re sinful.%u201d He expressed that he had no desire to tell people that %u201ctheir religion is wrong,%u201d as he would consider that just another form of colonialism. But he did acknowledge how he finds it %u201cincredibly difficult to find that loving community%u201d within this particular religious framework. His testament acknowledges the tension between seeking safety and visibility for queer Gaels, while also respecting the faiths that have shaped Gaelic communities and in which many have found strength and meaning. The change must come from within and in its own time, but, in the meantime, Te%u00e0rlach puts great care into trying to create %u201ca forcefield of protection%u201d around those who might feel otherwise ostracized by certain aspects of wider Gaelic culture. This has been a key value of An Taigh C%u00e9ilidh, the Gaelic Caf%u00e9 and community space he runs in Stornoway. The volunteers there endeavour to make it as welcoming and inclusive as possible and the effect of knowing a space like this exists, even from afar, was felt by at least one person on the Air Ch%u00e9ilidh call. They commented how %u201cas a queer autistic person, when welcomes are given to a group, I always assume that I'm excluded,%u201d but that it was %u201creally encouraging to attend this session%u201d and hear about initiatives such as An Taigh C%u00e9ilidh.Cha t%u00e9id n%u00ec sam bith san d%u00f2rn d%u00f9inteNothing can go into a closed fist%u00d2mar acknowledged how %u201cit%u2019s really tricky, coming from a colonized culture, to lose so much so fast. I think it%u2019s easy for people to feel like they want to hang on to what there is and really sort of guard that.%u201d But she also suggested how we might collectively re-author that narrative of defense, guardedness and clinging tight to things out of fear: %u201cOne of the things that I ask, and I wonder about, about things that are traditionally %u2018G%u00e0idhealach%u2019 . . . when did they come in, how were they born?
